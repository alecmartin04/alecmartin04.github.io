About Me

I am in the job market! Please reach out if my research areas of expertise can be an asset to your work!

I am currently a graduate research assistant at the University of Missouri - Columbia working under the supervision of Dr. Yicheng Guo. I am an observer that utilizes state-of-the-art space telescopes such as Hubble and James Webb to study galaxy formation and evolution across the cosmic time. My research focuses primarily on studying the nature of giant UV-Bright Star-Forming clumps that exist in ~50% of star-forming galaxies at cosmic noon (z~2). In brief, these clumps are dense regions of star-formation (or clusters of bright O, B type stars) that range in stellar mass from M ~ 10^6 - 10^8 M☉ and contribute ~10% of the total Ultraviolet light from their host galaxies. These substructures are believed to be key building blocks for the devolopment of morphological features such as a galactic bulge and gasous disc thickness. My other research interests broadly encompass: (1) Enviornmental effects both (in-situ and ex-situ) towards the suppression of star-formation, (2) Lensed galactic systems at high-redshift, and (3) Dwarf Galaxies Properties and Scaling Relations.

AGN Host Galaxies

Active Galactic Nuclei can influence the surpression of star-formation and thus impacting the formation of giant clumps within the host galatic disc. Recent work investigates the abundance of host galaxies of both AGNs and clumps within HST/CANDELS galaxies to test the relation between the two vital substructures and provide a clearer picture on how they impact their host galaxies evolution over time.

Dwarf Galaxies

Dwarf galaxies (M* < 10^9 M☉) have lower potential wells compared to higher mass galaxies making them more suseptable to star-fomration enhancing/surpressing processes. A major issue is that the scaling relations have high intrinsic scatter for dwarf galaxies. By studying the metalicity of these galaxies we can further constrain these relations and utilize dwarf galaxies and their burstiness (rapid star-forming events) to help characterise the structure of their dark matter haloes.

Gravitationally Lensed Systems

Gravitational lensing both weak and strong provide unique laboratories to study galactic substructures to sub-kiloparsec scales at higher redshifts than otherwise capable. Recent studies utilize such systems to study clumps down to tens of parsecs from JWST images at high redshifts. The most popular examples were studies involving the 'Sunrise Arc'(z~6), 'Cosmic Grapes' (z=6.07) and 'Cosmic Gems' (z~10.2)

High Redshift Spirals

JWST has recently confirmed serveral disk galaxies out to a redshift of z~6 and spiral galaxies out to z ~ 4. Our recent work finds a higher fraction of spiral galaxies than what is currently expected near a redshift of z ~ 4. Since sprials are believed to be late-stage morphologies for galxies, this work places higher constraints to star-formation efficiencies within our current models as we try to match simulations with our observations of the early universe.
